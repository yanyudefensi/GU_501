    Through machine learning, data visualization, and association rule analysis, I found a lot of interesting information. For example, the difference between the voting results of the American Electoral College and the public. The fixed support states of the Democrats and Republicans, and the influence of swing states on the presidential election. In addition, Trump's supporters mostly come from states with underdeveloped economies and few citizens, such as the Rust Belt and Central States. In other words, his main supporters are the victims of economic globalization, as well as workers. These interesting information have enhanced my understanding of the US presidential election.


    Unfortunately, I did not check the Internet for specific methodological presentations of the New York Times and other media outlets predicting the U.S. election, and there is very limited information on the 2016 U.S. election publicly available on the Internet (sensitive information implicating national security). Considering the source of the data, the fact that Trump's main supporters are not good at using the media to speak out and belong to the group of silent voters may be a significant reason for the failure of the presidential election predictions of the New York Times and a host of other media outlets. Whether the collected data is representative is a question that every data scientist needs to think about repeatedly. Identifying problems from the source data is something that requires data scientists to have a sufficient knowledge base of the field they work in.


    Read the literature to understand the domain in which you work. Repeatedly think about the reliability of the data sources before performing data analysis. This is a valuable lesson I learned from the project.
